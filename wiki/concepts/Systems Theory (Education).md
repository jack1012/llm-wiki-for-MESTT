# Systems Theory (Education)

## Definition
In the context of educational research and student retention, **Systems Theory** views a college or university as a complex, self-regulating social system composed of interacting subsystems (academic, social, administrative).

## Application in the Tinto Model
**[[Vincent Tinto]]** (1973) utilized systems theory to move away from "individual deficit" explanations of dropout. He argued that:
- **Interdependence**: A student's experience in one subsystem (e.g., social clubs) affects their performance in another (e.g., academic study).
- **Equilibrium**: Persistence is a state of equilibrium between the individual's inputs (goals, ability) and the system's outputs (integration, success).
- **System Departure vs. Institutional Departure**: A student might leave one "system" (a specific college) to enter another (transfer), which is a system-level success but an institutional-level attrition.

## Relevance to STT
The **[[Secondary-to-Tertiary Transition (STT)]]** is essentially the process of a student moving from the stability of one system (high school) into the complexity of another (university). Transition failure is often a failure of the "university system" to provide accessible interface points for new "inputs" (incoming students).
