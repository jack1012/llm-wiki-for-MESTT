# Epistemological and Cognitive Discontinuity

## Definition
**Epistemological and Cognitive Discontinuity** refers to the sharp break in the nature of mathematical knowledge and the mental processes required to master it during the **[[Secondary-to-Tertiary Transition]]**.

This concept captures the "shock" students experience when they move from secondary school mathematics (which is often procedural and intuitive) to university mathematics (which is axiomatic and formal).

## Key Dimensions
1. **The Nature of Knowledge (Epistemological)**:
    - **Secondary**: Mathematics as a set of rules for calculation.
    - **Tertiary**: Mathematics as a formal logical system where definitions and proofs are primary.
2. **Mental Processes (Cognitive)**:
    - **Secondary**: Recognizing patterns and applying algorithms.
    - **Tertiary**: Constructing logical arguments, handling multiple abstract variables, and "re-inventing" concepts through formal definitions.

## Role in the "Math Crisis"
**[[Miguel de Guzmán]]** (1998) identified this as the first and deepest layer of STT difficulties. If students cannot overcome this discontinuity, they often revert to **[[Surface Learning]]** (memorizing proofs without understanding), which eventually leads to failure in advanced courses.
