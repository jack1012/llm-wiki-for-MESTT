## Definition

Psychological Reductionism, in the context of higher education attrition research, refers to the tendency of early researchers (pre-1970s) to attribute student dropout entirely to individual psychological deficits — such as "lack of motivation," "low intelligence," or "poor personality fit." This approach treats the institution as a neutral backdrop and places full blame on the student for failing to persist.

## Theoretical Significance

The concept was systematically critiqued by [[Vincent Tinto]] in his 1973 foundational literature review. Tinto argued that this reductionist lens commits a fundamental attribution error: it confuses correlation (students who drop out may score lower on certain personality inventories) with causation (therefore their personality *caused* them to drop out). This critique directly motivated the development of the [[Student Integration Model]], which reframed dropout as an **interactional** failure between student and institution.

## STT Relevance

In the Secondary-to-Tertiary Transition (STT) context, Psychological Reductionism remains a persistent danger. When a first-year STEM student fails calculus and drops out, the reductionist interpretation would be "they lacked mathematical ability." The interactionist alternative — championed by Tinto (1973), [[Miguel de Guzmán]] (1998), and [[Ghislaine Gueudet]] (2008) — argues that the university's failure to bridge the cognitive-language gap, provide social integration, and adapt its didactical contract is equally responsible.
