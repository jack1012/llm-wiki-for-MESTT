## Definition

The Triple Taxonomy of Difficulties is [[Miguel de Guzmán]]'s (1998) comprehensive classification framework for the multi-dimensional challenges students face during the Secondary-to-Tertiary Transition (STT) in mathematics. Presented as a keynote at the [[International Congress of Mathematicians (ICM)]] in Berlin, this taxonomy argued that STT difficulty is not reducible to "mathematics being harder" but involves three distinct and interacting dimensions of disruption.

## The Three Dimensions

### 1. Socio-Cultural Shock (社會文化衝擊)
The transition from a controlled, familiar, small-classroom high school environment to the anonymous, massive, and self-directed university environment. Key elements include:
- Loss of personal attention from teachers
- Anonymity in large lecture halls (hundreds of students)
- Removal of the "safety net" of parental and teacher oversight
- Cultural alienation in a new city or institutional culture

See [[Socio-Cultural Shock]] for detailed analysis.

### 2. Methodological/Attitudinal Shift (學習態度與方法的斷層)
The fundamental change in what is expected of the learner:
- High school demands **obedience and reproduction** (follow instructions, memorize, reproduce).
- University demands **independence and construction** (self-regulate, discover, create).
- This shift in required agency catches students who are expert passive learners unprepared.

### 3. Cognitive-Language Barrier (認知與語言障礙)
The transformation of mathematical communication:
- High school mathematics uses an **intuitive, heuristic language** accessible to everyday reasoning.
- University mathematics uses a **strict, formalized, axiomatic language** that treats freshmen as if they were already mature mathematicians.
- This linguistic rupture makes even familiar content suddenly unintelligible.

See [[Cognitive-Language Barrier]] for detailed analysis.

## STT Relevance

The Triple Taxonomy is significant because it shows that STT difficulty is **always composite**. A student who struggles with proof (Dimension 3) will also struggle with the social isolation of not understanding what peers discuss (Dimension 1) and with the self-regulation demands of catching up independently (Dimension 2). The three dimensions are not additive but **multiplicative** — each amplifies the others.

This framework directly influenced [[Ghislaine Gueudet]]'s (2008) Three-Dimensional Framework (Cognitive-Social-Institutional), which can be seen as a theoretical refinement and operationalization of Guzmán's original taxonomy.
