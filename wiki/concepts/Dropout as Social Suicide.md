## Definition

"Dropout as Social Suicide" is [[Vincent Tinto]]'s foundational theoretical analogy (1975), drawing on Émile Durkheim's *Le Suicide* (1897) to reframe student dropout from higher education as a form of social death within the academic community. Just as Durkheim argued that individuals who lack integration into society's moral (normative) and social (relational) fabric are at elevated risk of suicide, Tinto proposed that students who fail to integrate into the university's academic and social systems undergo a parallel process of disengagement that culminates in departure.

## The Durkheimian Parallel

Durkheim identified two forms of insufficient integration leading to suicide:
- **Anomic (迷亂型)**: The individual cannot connect with society's value system and normative structure.
- **Egoistic (利己型)**: The individual lacks meaningful social bonds and relationships.

Tinto mapped these onto the university context:
- **Academic anomie**: The student cannot connect with the intellectual values and academic expectations of the university → failure of [[Academic Integration]].
- **Social egoism**: The student cannot form meaningful relationships with peers and faculty → failure of [[Social Integration]].

When both forms of integration fail simultaneously, the student experiences an "academic death" — complete withdrawal from the institution.

## STT Relevance

This analogy is particularly powerful in explaining first-year STT attrition. A high school student who excelled in mathematics enters a university where:
- The mathematical language has become unrecognizable ([[Cognitive-Language Barrier]]) → academic anomie
- The large, anonymous lecture halls offer no personal connection ([[Socio-Cultural Shock]]) → social egoism

The combination creates what [[Miguel de Guzmán]] (1998) later called the "composite shock" — and what Di Martino (2018) described as [[Mathematical Crisis]]. The student's departure is not a rational, voluntary decision; it is the inevitable result of systemic disintegration, just as Durkheim's suicide is not merely a personal choice but a structural outcome.
