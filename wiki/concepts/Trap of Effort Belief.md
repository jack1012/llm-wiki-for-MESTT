## Definition

The Trap of Effort Belief describes the psychological crisis that occurs when a student's deeply held belief that "hard work guarantees success" is suddenly invalidated by university-level failure. The concept was identified in [[Kathryn Crawford]]'s (1994) analysis of students who deployed high levels of effort through [[Surface Learning Approach]] strategies, only to find that increased effort produced worse, not better, outcomes. [[Pietro Di Martino]] (2018) later described this as the "Betrayal of Effort" — one of the primary triggers of [[Mathematical Crisis]].

## Mechanism

The trap operates through a three-stage process:

1. **Prior Reinforcement (高中階段的正增強)**: Throughout secondary school, the student's experience consistently confirms the equation: *Effort (memorization + practice) → High Grades*. This builds a robust implicit belief that effort is both necessary and sufficient for academic success.

2. **Strategy Failure (策略失效)**: Upon encountering university mathematics (proof-based, abstract, conceptually demanding), the student applies the same high-effort surface strategies. However, because these strategies are epistemologically misaligned with the demands (see [[Epistemological Mismatch]]), the effort fails to produce results.

3. **Attribution Crisis (歸因危機)**: The student cannot understand *why* they are failing despite unprecedented effort. Two devastating attributions emerge:
   - "I must not be smart enough" → self-concept collapse
   - "Mathematics has betrayed me" → [[Disciplinary Heartbreak]]

## STT Relevance

The Trap of Effort Belief is particularly destructive because it targets the **highest-performing** secondary school students — those most invested in the effort-reward equation. Students who coasted through high school with minimal effort may actually adapt *better* to university, because they never built the rigid belief structure that the trap exploits.

This concept bridges the cognitive and affective dimensions of STT:
- **Cognitive**: The failure is produced by [[Epistemological Mismatch]] (wrong learning strategy).
- **Affective**: The failure is *experienced* as betrayal, shame, and identity crisis (see [[Mathematical Crisis]], [[Learned Helplessness in Math]]).
