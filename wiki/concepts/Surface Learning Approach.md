## Definition

A Surface Learning Approach refers to a learning strategy characterized by rote memorization, mechanical reproduction, and the absence of meaning-seeking. In mathematics, a surface learner focuses on memorizing formulas, reproducing procedural steps, and pattern-matching to solve problems without understanding the underlying logic or conceptual structure.

The concept originates from the Marton & Säljö (1976) distinction between deep and surface approaches to learning, later operationalized in mathematics education by [[Kathryn Crawford]] (1994). Crawford demonstrated that surface approaches are directly produced by **fragmented** epistemological conceptions of mathematics (viewing math as a disconnected set of rules rather than a coherent logical system).

## Characteristics in Mathematics

- **Memorization over understanding**: Focuses on "what formula to use" rather than "why this formula works."
- **Procedure reproduction**: Can execute algorithms correctly but cannot adapt when the problem format changes.
- **Isolation of knowledge**: Treats each topic as independent, missing the relational structure of mathematical knowledge.
- **Exam-oriented**: Studies exclusively for assessment performance, not for intellectual growth.

## STT Relevance

Surface Learning Approach is highly relevant to STT because it is paradoxically *rewarded* in many secondary school systems. High school mathematics education — particularly in exam-driven systems like those in East Asia and parts of Europe — often inadvertently trains students to be expert surface learners. These students can achieve perfect scores through pattern recognition and formula memorization.

The crisis occurs at the university level, where:
- Proof-based courses require understanding, not reproduction.
- Novel problem formats punish pure memorization.
- The sheer volume of material makes rote learning unsustainable.

This creates the [[Epistemological Mismatch]] that Crawford identified, and feeds directly into the [[Trap of Effort Belief]]: students who double down on surface strategies in response to failure find that more effort produces *worse* results.

## Contrast with Deep Learning

See [[Deep Learning]] for the contrasting approach characterized by meaning-seeking, relational understanding, and intrinsic intellectual engagement.
