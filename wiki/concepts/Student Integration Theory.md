# Student Integration Theory (Tinto's Model)

## Definition
**Student Integration Theory**, developed by **[[Vincent Tinto]]** (1973, 1975, 1987), is a theoretical framework explaining college student persistence as a function of the "fit" between the student and the institution. The theory posits that the more a student is integrated into the academic and social systems of their college, the more likely they are to commit to their goals and the institution, and thus persist until graduation.

## The Dual Dimensions of Integration
The model identifies two primary channels of integration:
1. **[[Academic Integration in Higher Education]]**: Intellectual engagement, grade performance, and meaningful interaction with faculty and curricula.
2. **[[Social Integration in Higher Education]]**: Peer group interactions, sense of belonging, and participation in extracurricular or informal social activities.

## Evolution of the Model
- **Static Model (1973)**: Initially identified the importance of fit and used Durkheim’s analogy of social cohesion to explain attrition.
- **[[Longitudinal Process Model]] (1975)**: Refined the theory into a dynamic process where student experiences continuously refine their **[[Goal vs. Institutional Commitment]]**.

## Relevance to STT
In the **[[Secondary-to-Tertiary Transition]]**, this theory explains the "shock" many students experience. If a student is unable to achieve **Academic Integration** (e.g., they fail their first math midterm—the "**[[First-time Syndrome]]**") or **Social Integration** (e.g., they feel isolated in large lecture halls), their commitment to the institution drops, leading to early dropout.
