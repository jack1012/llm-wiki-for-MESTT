## Definition

Taxonomy of Attrition refers to the systematic classification of different forms of student departure from higher education. [[Vincent Tinto]] (1973) identified a critical flaw in the pre-1970s dropout literature: researchers were treating all forms of leaving as equivalent, when in fact they represent fundamentally different phenomena with distinct causes, processes, and outcomes.

## Key Categories

Tinto distinguished at least three distinct forms of departure:

1. **Dropout (永久退學)**: Permanent withdrawal from all higher education. This represents genuine transition failure — the student exits the system entirely.
2. **Stopout (暫時休學)**: Temporary leave with eventual return. This may represent strategic delay (accumulating financial resources) or a "benign pause" rather than failure.
3. **Transfer (轉學)**: Movement to another institution. Far from being a failure, transfer often reflects a mature reassessment of person-environment fit — the student is *refining* their transition, not abandoning it.

## STT Relevance

This taxonomy has profound implications for how we measure and interpret STT outcomes. A university that reports a 30% "dropout rate" may be conflating students who transferred to better-fitting programs (positive outcome), students who took a gap year to recover from burnout (neutral outcome), and students who permanently abandoned higher education (negative outcome). Without this distinction, institutional intervention strategies will inevitably misfire.

In the Taiwanese context, the distinction between "休學 (stopout)" and "退學 (dropout)" is equally critical, as highlighted by the National Academy for Educational Research (2020) report that warned against treating all institutional departures as failures.
