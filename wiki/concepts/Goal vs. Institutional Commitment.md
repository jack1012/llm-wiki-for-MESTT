# Goal vs. Institutional Commitment

## Definition
In **[[Vincent Tinto]]'s** (1975) longitudinal model of persistence, a critical distinction is made between two types of student commitment that drive the decision to persist or leave higher education:

1. **Goal Commitment**: The student’s individual commitment to achieving an educational goal (e.g., earning a bachelor's degree in mathematics). This is often tied to career aspirations and personal values.
2. **Institutional Commitment**: The student’s attachment to the **specific institution** they are attending. This reflects how much the student feels they "belong" at that particular college and whether its environment meets their needs.

## Why the Distinction Matters
These two commitments generate different types of departure:
- **Low Goal Commitment** → Often leads to **permanent dropout** from the higher education system entirely.
- **High Goal Commitment + Low Institutional Commitment** → Often leads to **Transfer** (the student still wants the degree but believes another institution is a better fit).
- **High Institutional Commitment + Low Academic Performance** → May lead to academic dismissal despite the student wanting to stay.

## Relevance to STT
During the **[[Secondary-to-Tertiary Transition]]**, many students maintain high **Goal Commitment** (they want to be engineers or scientists) but face a crisis in **Institutional Commitment** when they encounter large, anonymous classes or a "competitive culture" (**[[De Guzmán's categories|De Guzmán 1998]]**). Bridging programs aim to strengthen both by providing early academic success and building peer communities.
