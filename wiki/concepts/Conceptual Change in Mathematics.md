# Conceptual Change in Mathematics

## Definition
**Conceptual Change** refers to the cognitive process where a student must restructure their existing mental models or "conceptions" to accommodate new, often contradictory, information. In mathematics, this typically involves moving from **[[Elementary Mathematical Thinking]] (EMT)** (intuitive, practical) to **[[Advanced Mathematical Thinking]] (AMT)** (axiomatic, formal).

## The STT Conceptual Shift
In the **[[Secondary-to-Tertiary Transition]]**, conceptual change is often described as a "re-orientation" rather than just adding more knowledge:
- **From Calculation to Proof**: Students must stop seeing math as "getting the right number" and start seeing it as "validating a logical claim."
- **From Examples to Definitions**: In high school, an example might "be" the concept; in university, the formal definition is the primary source of truth, even if it contradicts intuition (**[[Concept Image vs Concept Definition]]**).

## Cognitive Barriers
- **[[Met-befores]]**: Previous knowledge that worked well in high school (e.g., "multiplication makes a number bigger") but becomes an obstacle in university math (e.g., when multiplying by fractions < 1).
- **Procedural Entrenchment**: The heavy training in surface-level procedures makes it difficult for students to "see" the underlying logic.
