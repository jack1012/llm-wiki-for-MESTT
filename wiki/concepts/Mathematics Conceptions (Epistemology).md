# Mathematics Conceptions (Epistemology)

## Definition
**Mathematics Conceptions** refer to the underlying qualitative beliefs and understandings students hold about the nature of mathematics—what it is, what counts as mathematical knowledge, and how it is validated. In STT research, these are often analyzed as an epistemological dimension that precedes and dictates **[[Learning Approaches]]**.

## Crawford’s Taxonomy (1994)
**[[Kevin Crawford]]** identified five distinct levels of conceptions among entering university students:
- **Level A**: Mathematics is a collection of fragmented rules and formulas.
- **Level B**: Mathematics is about applying those rules to solve specific problems.
- **Level C**: Mathematics is an abstract logical system.
- **Level D**: Mathematics is a logic system used as a powerful problem-solving tool.
- **Level E**: Mathematics is an integrated system for understanding the world.

## The Conception-Learning Link
Research suggests a causal link:
- **Naive/Fragmented Conceptions (A-B)** → Lead to **[[Surface Learning]]** (memorization, rote practice).
- **Complex/Cohesive Conceptions (C-E)** → Lead to **[[Deep Learning]]** (logical integration, searching for meaning).

## Relevance to STT
A core challenge of the **[[Secondary-to-Tertiary Transition]]** is the mismatch between the procedural focus of secondary school (training students in Level A-B conceptions) and the axiomatic/theoretical focus of university mathematics (requiring Level C-E conceptions). Failure to make this "epistemological shift" is a primary cause of **[[Cognitive Discontinuity]]**.
