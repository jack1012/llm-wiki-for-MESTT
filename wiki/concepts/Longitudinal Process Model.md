# Longitudinal Process Model

## Definition
The **Longitudinal Process Model**, formalized by **[[Vincent Tinto]]** in 1975, views college dropout not as a single event based on entry characteristics, but as a dynamic, unfolding process. It posits that a student’s daily experiences within the institution continuously modify their goals and commitments, leading ultimately to a decision to persist or leave.

## Key Evolution from earlier models
- **Dynamic Feedback Loops**: Unlike static "fit" models, the longitudinal model recognizes that high integration at one point can be undermined by later negative experiences, or weak entry characteristics can be overcome by strong university support.
- **Feedback Loop Example**: Successful performance in early exams → Increased academic integration → Higher **[[Goal vs. Institutional Commitment|Goal Commitment]]** → Increased effort → Continued persistence.

## Components of the Longitudinal Process
1. **Pre-entry Attributes**: Family background, skills, and prior schooling.
2. **Initial Goals/Commitments**: The student's starting motivation.
3. **Institutional Experiences**: Interactions with faculty and peers.
4. **Integration (Academic & Social)**: The core results of those experiences.
5. **Modified Goals/Commitments**: The revised desire to stay or graduate.
6. **Outcome**: The final decision (Stay, Transfer, Leave).

## Relevance to STT
Transition is inherently longitudinal. Research into **[[Secondary-to-Tertiary Transition]]** often utilizes this model to study **temporal changes** in student affect, motivation, and interest during their first year (**[[Sebastian Geisler|Geisler 2021]]**, **[[Michael Liebendoerfer|Liebendörfer 2023]]**).
