## Attribution Theory and Persistence in Secondary-Tertiary Transition

**Source**: Di Martino & Gregorio (2018), applying Weiner's attribution theory to STT context.

### Core Insight
How students **explain failure** (attribute causes) determines whether they persist or withdraw. Students experiencing first mathematical failure must construct explanatory narratives—and those narratives predict outcomes.

### Four Attribution Dimensions

Students can attribute failure to:

**1. LOCUS (Internal vs. External)**
- Internal: "I lack ability" or "I didn't try hard enough"
- External: "The course is poorly taught" or "The transition is hard"

**2. STABILITY (Stable vs. Unstable)**
- Stable: "I've never been good at proofs" (fixed trait)
- Unstable: "I had a bad week" (temporary condition)

**3. CONTROLLABILITY (Controllable vs. Uncontrollable)**
- Controllable: "I didn't study effectively" (can improve)
- Uncontrollable: "Math is inherently difficult for me" (cannot change)

**4. GLOBALITY (Global vs. Specific)**
- Global: "I'm not a math person" (affects all mathematics)
- Specific: "I struggle with proofs, but calculus is OK" (domain-limited)

### Persistence-Predicting Patterns

**LEADS TO PERSISTENCE:**
- External locus: "The course is hard, but that's normal in university"
- Unstable: "I've had a rough start, but can improve"
- Controllable: "I need better study strategies"
- Specific: "Proofs are hard, but other topics work for me"
- **Interpretation**: "I can improve by changing my approach"

**LEADS TO WITHDRAWAL:**
- Internal locus: "I lack ability in mathematics"
- Stable: "I've always struggled with mathematics"
- Uncontrollable: "I'm just not a math person"
- Global: "My mathematical mind doesn't work"
- **Interpretation**: "No amount of effort will fix this"

### Empirical Finding from DiMartino
Successful students (who persisted) commonly adopted **unstable, controllable, specific attributions**:
- "I need to adjust my study habits"
- "Proof writing is new; I'll learn"
- "My friends are struggling too; this is normal"

Dropout students adopted **stable, uncontrollable, global attributions**:
- "I'm not smart enough for university math"
- "I was never good at proofs; that's not how my mind works"
- "If I can't do this naturally, I should quit"

### Intervention Opportunity
Teaching attributional reframing can improve persistence:
- Help students shift from "I lack ability" to "I need different strategies"
- Normalize struggle as part of learning, not sign of incompetence
- Teach metacognitive awareness of attributional patterns
