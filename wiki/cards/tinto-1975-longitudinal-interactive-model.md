## Longitudinal Interactive Model of Dropout

**Source**: Tinto, V. (1975). Dropout from Higher Education: A Theoretical Synthesis of Recent Research. Review of Educational Research, 45(1), 89-125.

### Central Innovation
Tinto advances from his 1973 model by reconceptualizing dropout as a **dynamic process unfolding over time**, not a static binary outcome. The model emphasizes **feedback loops**: college experiences modify student goals and commitments, which then affect persistence decisions.

### Core Process Flow
```
Pre-College Background 
       ↓
Entry → Academic Integration → Goal/Commitment Modification
     ↘  Social Integration  ↙
                ↓
        Persistence Decision
        (Stay/Withdraw/Transfer)
```

### Key Temporal Insight
Student's commitment to degree and institution **changes over time** based on:
1. Quality of academic integration (grades, faculty interaction)
2. Quality of social integration (peer relationships, belonging)
3. Feedback loops: Success → higher commitment → persistence

### Why Longitudinal Matters for STT
Students transitioning from secondary to tertiary education face particularly vulnerable period:
- First semester: zero integration → high risk
- Gradually integrated → commitment increases
- Weak integration after 1-2 semesters → decision to leave

The model explains why high-achieving secondary students may struggle at start of tertiary (temporary low integration) but persist if institutions provide support (facilitate integration).

### Predictive Power
Unlike 1973's descriptive approach, longitudinal model allows:
- Early identification of at-risk students (low integration indicators)
- Intervention timing: when student commitment is malleable
- Understanding why students drop out even after successful first semester (delayed realization of poor fit)
