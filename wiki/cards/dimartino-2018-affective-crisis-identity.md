## Affective-Identity Crisis in STT for High Achievers

**Source**: Di Martino, P., & Gregorio, F. (2018). The Mathematical Crisis in Secondary–Tertiary Transition. International Journal of Science and Mathematics Education, 17, 825-843.

### Core Phenomenon: "Mathematical Crisis"
DiMartino's primary contribution is reframing STT difficulty as **fundamentally an affective-emotional crisis**, not merely a cognitive problem. For high-achieving students especially, the tertiary transition produces collision between secondary identity ("I am good at mathematics") and tertiary reality ("I don't understand anything").

### The "First-Time Phenomenon"
High-achievers simultaneously experience multiple "firsts":
- First time not best in their math class
- First time unable to understand material despite effort
- First time experiencing failure in mathematics
- First time doubt their mathematical ability

### Identity Rupture Mechanism
**Secondary Math Identity**: "Math comes naturally to me. I'm good at this. I can rely on my talent."

**Tertiary Shock**: Suddenly cannot keep up. Traditional strategies (memorization, procedural practice) don't work. Effort fails. Self-concept collapses.

**Emotional Cascade**: Confusion → Anxiety → Shame → Helplessness → (for some) Withdrawal

### Why High Achievers Vulnerable
Unlike weaker students who have **prior experience with difficulty and coping strategies**, high-achievers:
- Lack emotional scaffolding for failure
- Have no prior adaptive responses to struggle
- Experience identity threat ("Who am I without being 'the math person'?")
- Often feel shame talking about difficulty (perceived as personal failing)

### Contrast with Weaker Students
Weaker secondary students, who experienced struggle before, enter university:
- With realistic self-concept (not inflated)
- With prior coping strategies for difficulty
- Without identity threat (already knew math was hard)
- More likely to seek help without shame

**Paradox**: Secondary ability ≠ tertiary persistence. Identity and emotional resilience matter more.

### Intervention Implication
Cannot resolve affective crisis with cognitive support alone. Must:
- Normalize struggle (help students reframe failure as learning, not identity threat)
- Provide emotional support and peer community
- Challenge perfectionist narratives about mathematical talent
- Teach metacognitive coping strategies for mathematical difficulty
