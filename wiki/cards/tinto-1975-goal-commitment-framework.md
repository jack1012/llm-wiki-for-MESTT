## Goal and Institutional Commitment Framework

**Source**: Tinto, V. (1975). Dropout from Higher Education: A Theoretical Synthesis. Review of Educational Research, 45(1).

### Two Distinct Commitments
Tinto distinguishes **educational goal commitment** from **institutional commitment**—a critical refinement allowing more precise prediction of dropout.

### Educational Goal Commitment
- **Definition**: Intensity of student's desire to complete a college degree (general)
- **Basis**: Career aspirations, educational value, family expectations
- **Stability**: Relatively stable across time
- **Predictive Value**: Determines whether student will persist *somewhere* in higher ed system
- **Low Goal Commitment** → High risk of permanent dropout from higher education

### Institutional Commitment
- **Definition**: Specificity of attachment to *this particular institution*
- **Basis**: Financial investment, geographic convenience, prestige, institutional fit
- **Stability**: Malleable; can change based on college experience
- **Predictive Value**: Determines whether student will stay at current institution or transfer
- **Low Institutional Commitment** → Transfer to other institution (not system dropout)

### Empirical Implications
```
High Goal + High Institution Commitment  → Persist (complete degree here)
High Goal + Low Institution Commitment   → Transfer (seek better fit elsewhere)
Low Goal  + High Institution Commitment  → Voluntary withdrawal (leave education)
Low Goal  + Low Institution Commitment   → Voluntary withdrawal (no education motivation)
```

### Cost-Benefit Integration
Financial investment affects institutional commitment:
- **High-cost private institutions**: Financial sunk costs increase institutional commitment → higher persistence
- **Low-cost public institutions**: Easy exit → lower persistence for low-integration students
- **First-generation students** (often low-SES): May have high goal commitment but low institutional commitment (due to alienation)

### Relevance to STT
Secondary-to-tertiary transition can modify both commitments:
- Early difficulty might lower institutional commitment (but not goal commitment)
- If institution provides integration support → commitment rises → persistence
- Without support → commitment stays low → transfer or withdrawal
