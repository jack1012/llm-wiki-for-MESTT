## Five Categories of Mathematics Conceptions (Crawford 1994)

**Source**: Crawford, K., Gordon, S., Nicholas, J., & Prosser, M. (1994). Conceptions of Mathematics and How It Is Learned. Learning and Instruction, 4(4), 331-345.

### Core Taxonomy
Crawford's phenomenographic analysis identified five qualitatively distinct ways students conceive of mathematics:

#### **Level A (Procedural/Fragmented)**
- Math = Rules, Procedures, Formulas
- Learning approach: Memorization
- Risk: Cannot transfer knowledge; fails on novel problems
- Prevalence: **Most common** among entering university students (esp. high school trained)

#### **Level B (Rules + Application)**
- Math = Rules applied to solve problems
- Learning approach: Surface learning with instrumental purpose
- Risk: Understands when to apply rule, but not why rule works
- Prevalence: Significant portion of first-year cohort

#### **Level C (Abstract Logic)**
- Math = Complex logical system; abstract entities with properties
- Learning approach: Shifting toward deep learning
- Risk: May understand logic but struggle connecting to applications
- Prevalence: Smaller minority

#### **Level D (Logic + Problem-Solving)**
- Math = Logical system + tool for problem-solving
- Learning approach: Deep, integrated understanding
- Risk: Minimal; students at this level typically succeed
- Prevalence: ~10-15% of entering cohort

#### **Level E (Insight/Modeling)**
- Math = Complex system for understanding world; source of insight and truth
- Learning approach: Deep, reflective engagement
- Risk: Minimal; highest mathematical maturity
- Prevalence: **Rare** among first-year students (<5%)

### Why This Matters for STT

**Secondary-to-Tertiary Transition involves conception shift**:
- High school math trains Level A-B thinking (procedures, rules, calculations)
- University math requires Level C-E thinking (logic, proof, abstraction)
- Students cannot simply "apply higher-school rules"; must **reconceive** what mathematics is
- Mismatch → cognitive dissonance → struggle/withdrawal

### Empirical Pattern
Students entering university cluster at A-B levels. Those who persist must transition toward C-E over first year. Those who fail to shift risk dropout or poor grades.
