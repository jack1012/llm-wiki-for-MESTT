## Durkheim Anomie Analogy for Student Dropout

**Source**: Tinto, V., & Cullen, J. (1973). Drawing from Émile Durkheim's *Le Suicide* (1897).

### Theoretical Bridge: Suicide → Dropout

Tinto adapts Durkheim's sociological theory of suicide to explain college dropout:

**Durkheim's Argument (Suicide)**:
- Suicide results from **anomie** (normlessness, social disconnection)
- Individuals with weak integration into society → higher suicide risk
- Social integration = protective factor

**Tinto's Application (Dropout)**:
- Dropout analogous to suicide (both extreme forms of disengagement)
- Student dropout results from **anomie in institutional context**
- Students with weak academic AND social integration → higher dropout risk
- Institutional integration = protective factor

### Two Forms of Integration

**Academic Integration** (Intellectual Anomie Prevention)
- Student becomes integrated into intellectual community
- Involves faculty interaction, academic engagement, intellectual challenge
- Signals: "I belong in this academic world"

**Social Integration** (Social Anomie Prevention)
- Student becomes integrated into peer/campus community
- Involves friendships, social participation, sense of belonging
- Signals: "I belong in this social world"

### Why Both Matter
Anomie ≠ single form of isolation. Complete integration requires **both**:
- Only academic success without friends = still at risk
- Only social belonging without academic success = still at risk
- Both integrated = resilient

### Implications
- Institutions prevent dropout by creating conditions for BOTH integration types
- Residential life, small classes, peer advising, faculty mentoring all serve integration
- Policy insight: Expand access to higher education without support systems = anomie → attrition
