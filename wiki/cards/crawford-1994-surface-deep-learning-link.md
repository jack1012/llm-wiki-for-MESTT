## Surface Learning vs. Deep Learning in Mathematics

**Source**: Crawford et al. (1994), building on Marton's phenomenographic framework.

### The Learning Approach Framework
Marton's distinction (adapted to mathematics):

**Surface Learning Approach**:
- Memorize rules and procedures without understanding principles
- Treat mathematical knowledge as isolated facts
- Focus on passing exams through memorization
- Outcome: Cannot transfer to novel problems; fragile knowledge
- Typical of students with **Level A-B conceptions**

**Deep Learning Approach**:
- Seek to understand underlying logic and principles
- Integrate new knowledge with existing understanding
- Ask "why" questions about mathematical structure
- Outcome: Can transfer knowledge; flexible problem-solving
- Typical of students with **Level C-E conceptions**

### Conception-Learning Link (Core Finding)
**A student's conception of what mathematics IS directly determines their learning approach**:
- Conception of math as "rules" → Memorization approach
- Conception of math as "logical system" → Understanding approach

This is not about student ability, but **epistemic belief** (what counts as valid mathematical knowledge).

### STT Vulnerability
First-year students often persist with surface approaches despite university context requiring depth. This mismatch creates:
- Poor grades despite effort (memorized procedures don't work for proof-based problems)
- Frustration (feeling incompetent when in fact just using wrong approach)
- Potential withdrawal (if no cognitive shift occurs)

### Intervention Implication
To improve STT outcomes: Change conceptions → change learning approaches → improve outcomes. This is why teaching about mathematical proof, abstraction, and reasoning (not just procedures) is critical for transition support.
