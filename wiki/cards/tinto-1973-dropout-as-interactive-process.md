## Dropout as Interactive Process

**Source**: Tinto, V., & Cullen, J. (1973). Dropout in Higher Education: A Review and Theoretical Synthesis of Recent Research.

### Core Insight
Tinto's critical reframing: Student dropout is **neither individual failure nor institutional failure**, but emerges from **poor fit between student characteristics and institutional environment**. This interactive perspective challenges deficit-based thinking.

### Traditional vs. Interactive Model

**Traditional (Deficit) View**:
- Dropout = student lacks ability/motivation
- Responsibility placed on individual
- Institution assumes it is functioning correctly

**Interactive View (Tinto)**:
- Dropout = mismatch in student-institution interaction
- **Both** student and institution contribute
- Institutional design/climate matters as much as student characteristics

### Process Model
```
Student Background → Entry → Integration → Commitment → Persistence/Attrition
  (SES, Ability)      ↓         ↓              ↓
                    Environment: Academic & Social  
```

### Key Variables Mediating the Process
1. **Student Entry Characteristics**: Pre-college ability, motivation, social status
2. **Institutional Environment**: Academic rigor, social climate, residential arrangements
3. **Integration Process**: How well student fits into academic/social community
4. **Institutional Commitment**: Student's emotional investment in the college

### Why This Matters for Policy & Practice
- Suggests that improving retention requires **institutional change**, not just student recruitment
- Points to importance of social/residential systems, academic advising, peer interaction
- Explains why some "capable" students leave (poor fit) and "less capable" students persist (good fit)
