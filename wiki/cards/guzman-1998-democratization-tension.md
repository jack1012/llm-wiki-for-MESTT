## Card: Democratization-Quality Tension in Higher Education

**Source**: De Guzmán, Hodgson, Robert & Villani (1998)

### The Paradox

**1980s-1990s Policy Goal**: Democratize higher education access (open universities to working-class, underrepresented students)

**Unintended Consequence**: System designed for homogeneous, high-achieving students suddenly must serve heterogeneous, lower-prepared populations—**but infrastructure doesn't change**.

### The Mismatch

**What Happens**:
- Weak students entering university have **critical need**: high-touch, personalized instruction, multiple explanations, concrete examples
- **What they get**: Large lecture halls (100+ students), TAs of dubious quality, office hours too few to accommodate demand, minimal written materials

### De Guzmán's Observation

> "Many weak students are getting access to university. For such students, their relation to knowledge is often not up to what is being expected of them: they meet difficulties in reaching the required level of abstraction and they confine themselves to mere actions and applications of recipes, unaware of the conceptual shift they must accomplish. As a result of this fragility, these students are in a great need of a highly personalized relationship with their instructor, which would allow for the numerous explanations they require. **But the current structure of university makes almost impossible such a contact.**"

### The Dilemma

**Option A**: Lower standards to match student ability
- ✓ More students succeed
- ✗ Degree becomes less valuable; no preparation for rigor students will need

**Option B**: Maintain standards, let weak students fail
- ✓ Degree prestige preserved
- ✗ High dropout rates; perpetuates class inequities

**Option C**: Redesign the system for diverse learners
- ✓ Maintains rigor while supporting diversity
- ✗ Requires institutional commitment, faculty development, resources (rarely prioritized)

### The Implicit System Assumption

Traditional university teaching assumes:
- "Average" student = excellent secondary school graduate from elite track
- Everyone can learn from pure lectures
- Support is remedial (for those who are deficient), not core

**But reality**: Incoming cohort is now highly diverse, with varied backgrounds and preparation.

### What De Guzmán Does NOT Say (But Implies)

This is **not** a student problem. This is an **institutional design problem**. Universities cannot simultaneously:
1. Admit heterogeneous students (democratization goal)
2. Maintain unchanged pedagogical practices (research-focused, large-scale)
3. Achieve high completion rates (quality goal)

At least one must change.

### Policy Implications

De Guzmán proposes **institutional responsibility** (later echoed by Tinto 2002):
- University must adapt to students, not expect students to adapt to university
- This may require:
  - Smaller class sizes in first year
  - Bridging programs
  - Peer tutoring and study groups
  - Faculty development in teaching
  - Integrated advising/support

### Historical Note

This tension **remains unresolved** in 2024. Many universities still operate with 1950s pedagogy but 2020s student diversity.
