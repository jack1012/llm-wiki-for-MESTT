## Student Integration Theory

**Source**: Tinto, V., & Cullen, J. (1973). Dropout in Higher Education: A Review and Theoretical Synthesis of Recent Research. Teachers College, Columbia University.

### Core Definition
Tinto's foundational theoretical framework proposing that student persistence results from the interactive fit between **individual characteristics** (ability, motivation, SES) and **institutional environment** (academic and social integration). Drawing from Durkheim's sociology, dropout occurs when students lack sufficient integration into the academic and social communities of their institution.

### Key Concept
**Integration** = strength of student's connections to both the academic (classroom, coursework, faculty interaction) and social (peer relationships, campus involvement) dimensions of institutional life.

- **High Integration** → Stronger institutional commitment → Persistence
- **Low Integration** → Weak commitment → Higher attrition risk

### Academic vs. Social Integration
- **Academic Integration**: Course performance, interaction with faculty, intellectual engagement
- **Social Integration**: Peer friendships, participation in campus life, sense of belonging
- **Both dimensions matter**: Either alone insufficient for persistence; synergistic effect

### Relevance to STT (Secondary-to-Tertiary Transition)
This theory directly explains STT difficulties: new university environment requires *both* academic (new rigor, proof-based reasoning) and social (new peer culture, institutional norms) integration simultaneously. Weak integration in either domain increases transition vulnerability.

### Historical Impact
- Foundational model for 50+ years of higher education research
- Basis for institutional retention initiatives (advising, mentoring, community-building)
- Applied across diverse institutional contexts (research universities, community colleges, international contexts)
