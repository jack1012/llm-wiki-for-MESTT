- **Problem**: Does the transition to formal university mathematics help students develop the "correct" (dynamic/process-oriented) beliefs required for success?
- **Method**: Longitudinal tracking of students' belief scores between the first week (T1) and mid-term (T2).
- **Conclusion**: The adjustment is **dysfunctional**. Successful transition would require an increase in dynamic beliefs, but results show a significant *decrease* in dynamic beliefs across the sample. For unsuccessful students (those who eventually dropped out), this decrease was significantly sharper, and their static beliefs (math as formulas) often *increased*.

This suggests that rather than the environment encouraging a more flexible view of mathematics, the shock of formal proofs and high-stakes exams causes students to retreat into rigid, school-grown schematic patterns. Instead of learning to value the "doing" of math, they become obsessed with "remembering" rules to survive, which perversely increases their dropout risk.
