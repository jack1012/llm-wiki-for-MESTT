## Practical-Theoretical Thinking Duality in Mathematics

**Source**: Gueudet, G. (2008), building on Tall's Advanced Mathematical Thinking framework.

### Core Theoretical Distinction

Mathematicians operate simultaneously in two modes:

**PRACTICAL THINKING**:
- Works with specific examples, prototypes, concrete cases
- Uses intuition, mental models, visual reasoning
- Basis: "Let me think of an example..."
- Strength: Builds intuition, accessible to learners
- Risk: Leads to overgeneralization if alone

**THEORETICAL THINKING**:
- Works with definitions, axioms, abstract structures, formal proofs
- Uses logical deduction, formal symbolism, rigorous argument
- Basis: "Let me prove this holds in all cases..."
- Strength: Provides certainty, handles infinite/abstract cases
- Risk: Can be disconnected from intuition if alone

### The Duality Requirement
Mature mathematicians **flexibly switch between modes**:
- Start with practical examples to build intuition
- Shift to theoretical proof to establish certainty
- Return to practical examples to interpret/apply

### STT Crisis: Loss of Duality
**Secondary school math** → trains practical thinking primarily
- Emphasis: procedural skill, worked examples, pattern recognition
- Students develop: strong intuitive/practical mode
- Missing: Theoretical mode completely absent or underdeveloped

**University math** → requires both modes, especially theoretical
- Emphasis: proof-based reasoning, abstraction, rigor
- Students lack: Theoretical thinking apparatus
- Result: Cannot flex between modes → rigid, incomplete understanding

### Why "Just Teach Proof" Fails
If university only emphasizes theoretical mode without practice mode:
- Students memorize proofs without intuition
- Fail to see why theorem matters
- Cannot transfer to new contexts
- **Better approach**: Explicitly teach mode-switching; use practical examples to motivate theoretical moves

### STT Implication
Effective transition support must teach students to develop theoretical thinking **while maintaining practical thinking**—creating flexible duality, not replacing one with another.
