## Core Claims (English Only)

### 1. Problematic
Research on the school-to-university transition in mathematics (STT) has proliferated, but often operates within a set of implicit assumptions and "common sense" discourses. These discourses—regarding student deficits, institutional gaps, and cognitive ruptures—may limit the field's ability to see the transition in a more socio-political and systemic light.

### 2. Research Problem
What are the dominant discourses being produced and reproduced in the STT research literature, and how do these discourses form a "discursive network" that shapes our understanding of student failure and success?

### 3. Key Results
Using an **intensive reading** approach, the authors identify a network of circulating discourses that frame STT primarily as a problem of individual adjustment or institutional mismatch. The study argues that these discourses often "naturalize" student struggles by focusing on cognitive development and educational background, while obscuring the socio-economic and political dimensions of the transition. The paper calls for a "reflexive turn" in STT research to unpack how our own scholarly discourses might be contributing to the very barriers we aim to dismantle.

### 4. Methodology
Qualitative synthesis and discourse analysis of a broad sample of STT literature. The authors employ an "intensive reading" method to map the connections between themes like social factors, economic context, and mathematical achievement, uncovering the underlying discursive structures.
