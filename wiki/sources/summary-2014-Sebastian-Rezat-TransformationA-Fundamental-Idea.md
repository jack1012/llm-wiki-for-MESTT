## Core Claims (English Only)
- **Problematic**: Mathematics education research is fragmented into diverse theories; "Transformation" is proposed as a unifying fundamental idea to bridge these theories.
- **Research Problem**: How do mathematical objects, representations, knowledge, and people's identities transform across educational contexts (especially during STT)?
- **Key Results**:
    1. STT is not just a gap in knowledge but a transformation from phenomenon-oriented to formal-language mathematics (Deiser & Reiss).
    2. Textbooks act as mediators in the "Didactic Tetrahedron," but there is a massive structural gap between school and university versions (Vollstedt et al.).
    3. Transition involves life-long identity work, moving from passive observer to active producer (Grevholm).
- **Methodology**: Multi-perspective; includes historical analysis, qualitative case studies, quantitative longitudinal surveys (TEDS-Telekom), and theoretical modeling.

## Structured Reading Notes
- **Part I: Transformations at Transitions**: Focuses heavily on STT and the "Double Discontinuity" (Klein).
- **Theoretical Contribution**: The **Didactic Tetrahedron** model (Rezat) places the artifact (textbook/tool) as a central vertex of interaction.
- **Main Findings**: University math's "speed and density" force a transformation of learning strategies. Students who rely on school "habits" (synonyms/synoptic descriptions) fail at the rigorous definitions required at university.
- **Discussion / Interpretation**: Transformation is "radical change" in properties. In STT, this means a "gestalt switch" in how one perceives the nature of mathematical truth.
