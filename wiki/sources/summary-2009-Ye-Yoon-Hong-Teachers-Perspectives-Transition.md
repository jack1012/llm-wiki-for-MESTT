## Core Claims (English Only)
- **Problematic**: There is a widening gap between secondary and tertiary mathematics competence, yet secondary teachers often lack a clear understanding of university requirements and expectations.
- **Research Problem**: What are secondary teachers' perceptions of the differences between school and university mathematics, and how do they view the role of calculus as bridging content?
- **Key Results**: Teachers exhibit a significant "ignorance" regarding tertiary teaching practices (60% didn't know if calculus is taught differently). They tend to "teach to the test" (NCEA) and focus on lower levels of achievement, which conflicts with the whole-subject mastery required at university.
- **Methodology**: Mixed-methods; Questionnaires (N=178) and semi-structured interviews with secondary calculus teachers in New Zealand.

## Structured Reading Notes
- **Research Topic**: Secondary teachers' awareness and perspectives on the STT.
- **Problem Awareness / Puzzle**: While research often blames students' lack of preparation, this study shifts the lens to teachers. Why do teachers who prepare students for university know so little about what happens there?
- **Research Questions**: 1) How do teachers perceive differences in assessment, style, and resources? 2) What is their view on the importance of calculus?
- **Theoretical Framework / Perspective**: Tall’s (2004, 2008) "Three Worlds of Mathematics" (Embodied, Symbolic, Formal).
- **Core Concepts**: Teacher Perceptions, Communication Gap, NCEA Assessment, Three Worlds of Math.
- **Data / Participants**: 178 New Zealand secondary teachers; 55% had taught calculus for >11 years.
- **Main Findings**: A "don't know" culture prevails among teachers regarding university math. Teachers prioritize passing exams (achieved/merit) over deep understanding (excellence). University math is seen as more "formal" and "teacher-centered" with less interaction.
- **Discussion / Interpretation**: The transition is a change of "learning cultures" (hands-on to hands-off). Improved communication between sectors is critical.
- **Contribution**: Highlights that the "Mathematics Problem" (LMS, 1995) is partly sustained by the isolation of secondary teaching from tertiary reality.
- **Suggestions / Implications**: Train lecturers to understand student knowledge; educate secondary teachers on tertiary requirements.
