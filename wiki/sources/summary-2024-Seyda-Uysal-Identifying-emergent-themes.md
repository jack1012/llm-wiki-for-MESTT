## Core Claims (English Only)
- **Problematic**: Very few studies address gender and marginalization from the standpoint of STT research, especially in the US.
- **Research Problem**: How do women and other marginalized students experience the affective aspects of STT?
- **Key Results**: Marginalized students experience STT as a challenge to their "identity." Women, in particular, often feel like "outsiders" in math even when they are high-achievers. The "Big-Fish-Little-Pond" effect is a major shock to their confidence.
- **Methodology**: Qualitative; Pilot study; Interviews & Seminars (N=13 students); Di Martino & Zan’s Three-dimensional Model of Attitude (TMA).

## Structured Reading Notes
- **Research Topic**: Marginalized students' affective transition.
- **Problem Awareness / Puzzle**: Why do women feel they must do "identity work" to participate as legitimate actors in math?
- **Theoretical Framework / Perspective**: TMA (Vision of Math, Perceived Competence, Emotional Disposition); Intersectionality.
- **Core Concepts**: Marginalization, Identity Work, Big-Fish-Little-Pond Effect (BFLPE), Relational vs Instrumental Vision.
- **Data / Participants**: Women and non-White students at a US research university.
- **Main Findings**: Pure Math (PM) majors find proofs "joyous" (like a puzzle), while Math Teaching (SMT) majors find them a "burden" due to perceived lack of utility. High grades do not always equate to high confidence for women.
- **Discussion / Interpretation**: US transition happens over time (due to liberal arts components) rather than as a sudden shock, unlike in Germany.
- **Contribution**: Highlights the "Exclusionary Identities" prevalent in mathematical spaces.
- **Suggestions / Implications**: Create formal and informal "mathematical communities" to support identity development for marginalized students.
