## Core Claims (English Only)

### 1. Problematic
In many developing educational contexts, such as Bangladesh, university mathematics departments face high failure rates and low student engagement. Existing research often fails to contrast the differing perceptions of students and teachers, leading to a disconnect between pedagogical interventions and student needs.

### 2. Research Problem
What are the primary factors contributing to success and failure in undergraduate mathematics from the dual perspectives of students and teachers, and how do these perceptions differ across gender and academic background?

### 3. Key Results
The study identifies a misalignment: (1) Students attribute failure to poor teaching methods, lack of resources, and external distractions, while (2) Teachers emphasize students' lack of conceptual understanding, aptitude, and motivation. Key success drivers included consistent practice, conceptual comprehension, and effective learning strategies. Notably, gender differences were found in the types of challenges reported (e.g., female students cited heavy course loads and lack of real-world connections).

### 4. Methodology
Mixed-methods approach: Likert-scale questionnaires and semi-structured interviews with undergraduate mathematics students and faculty in Bangladesh. Quantitative data analyzed using descriptive and inferential statistics.
