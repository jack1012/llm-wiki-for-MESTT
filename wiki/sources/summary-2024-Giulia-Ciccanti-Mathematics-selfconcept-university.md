## Core Claims (English Only)

### 1. Problematic
The transition from secondary to university mathematics is a high-risk period for student dropout. While cognitive difficulties are well-documented, the role of "mathematical self-concept"—specifically how its stability or change during the first year relates to the intention to leave—needs further investigation to identify early warning signs of departure.

### 2. Research Problem
How does the mathematics self-concept of first-year students evolve during the transition, and what is the relationship between the components of self-concept (internal vs. external) and a student's willingness to drop out?

### 3. Key Results
The study identifies a critical distinction between **internal** and **external** components of self-concept. Students whose self-concept is rooted in internal factors (e.g., personal enjoyment, ease of comprehension) exhibit higher stability and lower dropout intentions. Conversely, students whose self-concept relies on external factors (e.g., grades, external validation) are more susceptible to negative shifts during the first year. Among students considering dropping out (approx. 15% of the sample), 78% had a self-concept in secondary school primarily based on external factors, suggesting that externally-driven self-concept is a significant predictor of transition failure.

### 4. Methodology
Quantitative study conducted with first-year mathematics students at the **University of Ferrara**, Italy. The researchers used questionnaires to measure self-concept at the beginning and middle of the first year, tracking changes and dropout intentions.
