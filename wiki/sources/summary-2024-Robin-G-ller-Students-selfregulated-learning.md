## Core Claims (English Only)
- **Problematic**: 2/3 of university math time is self-study, but many students lack the SRL skills to handle it.
- **Research Problem**: How do different elements of the learning environment (lectures, exercises, peers) influence students' SRL processes?
- **Key Results**: Student regulation is mediated by their "appraisals" of task relevance and coping potential. Finnish environments provide better scaffolding for students with "survival" goals, whereas German environments often force students into "well-being" (ego-protection) mode due to high pressure.
- **Methodology**: Qualitative; Case-based; Interviews (N=6 students across 3 environments); Boekaerts’ dual processing model of SRL.

## Structured Reading Notes
- **Research Topic**: Institutional influence on SRL.
- **Problem Awareness / Puzzle**: Why do some students give up learning goals and switch to "just surviving"?
- **Theoretical Framework / Perspective**: Boekaerts' Dual Processing Model (Growth vs Well-being); Scherer’s Component Process Model (Appraisals).
- **Core Concepts**: Self-Regulated Learning (SRL), Appraisal, Growth vs Wellbeing, Institutional Scaffolding.
- **Data / Participants**: Math majors and pre-service teachers from Klagenfurt (Austria/Germany context) and Turku (Finland).
- **Main Findings**: Peer collaboration is the most "congruent" element across all environments. Assessment hurdles (e.g., the 50% homework rule) can inhibit SRL by forcing students to prioritize "points" over "understanding."
- **Discussion / Interpretation**: Finnish "Extreme Apprenticeship" reduces the cost of SRL by providing a safe space for struggle.
- **Contribution**: Connects institutional design directly to the psychology of self-regulation.
- **Suggestions / Implications**: Design learning environments with high coping potential (peers/tutors) to keep students in the "Growth" track.
