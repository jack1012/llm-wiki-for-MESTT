## Core Claims (English Only)

- **Problematic**: High failure and dropout rates in undergraduate mathematics suggest that previous secondary school success is not a reliable guarantor of university performance. Traditional "school-related" resources may not match university demands.

- **Research Problem**: To what extent do individual school-related resources (general prior knowledge, interest, self-concept) predict success in the first semester? Is the transition a continuous progression or a structural break?

- **Key Results**: Traditional school resources have only a **marginal influence** on first-semester success. General grades in school math are weak predictors. Instead, **specific precursory knowledge** (aligned with formal scientific standards of university math) and the ability to adopt **new learning strategies** are critical. The study also confirms a significant **Self-Concept Decline** as students shift from being the "best in class" in school to encountering a more rigorous peer group and content at university.

- **Methodology**: Quantitative longitudinal study (N=356) at a German university. Analyzed correlations between entry-level variables (grades, interest, self-concept) and first-semester exam results.

- **[[Person-Environment Fit]]** — The degree of congruence between student characteristics and the university environment; a key framework for STT attrition.
- **[[Scientific Mathematical Knowledge]]** — Formal, abstract, and rigorous knowledge required at university, distinct from procedural "school math."
- **[[Self-Concept Decline]]** — The psychological drop as students move from a "big fish" (school) to a "small fish" (university) status.
