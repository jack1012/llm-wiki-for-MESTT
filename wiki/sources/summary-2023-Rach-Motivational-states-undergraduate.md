## Core Claims (English Only)

### 1. Problematic
While the importance of motivation in STT is recognized, research often treats it as a stable trait, ignoring the situational fluctuations (states) that occur during specific learning activities. Understanding the relative weight of "who the student is" (person-specificity) versus "what the situation is" (situation-specificity) is crucial for designing effective classroom interventions.

### 2. Research Problem
To what extent do task values and student effort fluctuate between different learning situations in an undergraduate mathematics course, and how are these states predicted by stable individual characteristics versus situational perceptions?

### 3. Key Results
The study (N=181) reveals that task values and effort in advanced linear algebra courses exhibit high stability and only **slight fluctuation** across different situations. Motivational states are **strongly predicted by individual characteristics** (e.g., general individual interest in university mathematics) rather than by the specific situational context of a lesson. This suggests that "who the student is" when they enter the classroom is a more powerful driver of their engagement than the immediate instructional variables, highlighting the long-term impact of early interest development.

### 4. Methodology
Quantitative study using a person-oriented approach. 181 undergraduate students were surveyed at three time points across four separate lessons. Data were analyzed using linear mixed models to partition variance between persons (traits) and situations (states).
