## Core Claims (English Only)
- **Problematic**: University mathematics education practices (teaching and assessment) are underpinned by "mathematical ableism," which constructs disabled students as "abnormal" and "lesser" mathematicians.
- **Research Problem**: How are the subjectivities of disabled mathematics students shaped by the discursive space of ableism in university settings?
- **Key Results**: Students are shaped into "disabled subjects" through four characteristics: speed (the fast/slow student), disaffection (the stoic/emotional student), individualism (the solitary/communal student), and measurability (the measurable/intangible student).
- **Methodology**: Qualitative; Foucauldian discourse analysis; Narrative interviews with 9 university students (Finland) diagnosed with learning disabilities or mental health issues.

## Structured Reading Notes
- **Research Topic**: Subjectivity and ableism in university mathematics.
- **Problem Awareness / Puzzle**: While math is often seen as a safe haven for "odd people," it actually enforces a very narrow "norm" of the "able mathematician."
- **Research Questions**: How does university mathematics education contribute to "disabling" students?
- **Theoretical Framework / Perspective**: Foucauldian concepts of Subjectification, Dividing Practices, and Normalization; Disability Studies (Ableism).
- **Core Concepts**: Mathematical Ableism, Subjectification, Counter-conduct, Disability adjustments.
- **Data / Participants**: 9 Finnish university students (dyslexia, depression, etc.); high-achieving but marginalized.
- **Main Findings**: The "fast student" is the norm; "slowness" is pathologized. The "chalkboard" is a site of normalization and distress. Individualism is forced; asking for help is framed as a sign of disability.
- **Discussion / Interpretation**: Disability is not an attribute but a product of power relations in the classroom. Students exhibit "counter-conduct" by embracing slowness or creating alternative learning spaces.
- **Contribution**: Breaks the silence in math education research regarding the lived experiences of disabled students beyond remediation.
- **Suggestions / Implications**: University math communities should use "dis" (disability) as a way to "disrupt" oppressive orders and create inclusive pedagogies rather than just adjustments.
